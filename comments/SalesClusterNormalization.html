Feedback

This is a substantial advancement in the design. There’s a lot more here to comment on, so the volume of feedback is going to be greater….

Relation representation

The relation lines you are using have no tail and an arrowhead at the tip. I can make guesses about the meanings of these, but it would be more idiomatic to use the convention of a crow’s foot if you have a -many relation. It’s nice if you use the “one” indication also (represented by the short crossing line), but I will assume that if there’s no decoration on an end.

I’ll try to be clear when I’ve made a guess about the meaning of a relation.

Sale[s] cluster

Merchandise

I am thinking this is where you are modeling the items that are available for sale (assuming they are in stock?). If this is the right interpretation:

  • Name/price/description are all clearly describing the item
  • Inventory is good (it’s the stock level)
  • Date is confusing to me. It could be “date of last order” or “date of last inventory,” but nothing like that appears on the sample documemnts, so I think I would remove it.

There is a relation from Merchandise to Payment: if that is part of “how we last bought this item,” that is beyond the requirements of the database.

If the Payment is supposed to record what merchandise it was paying for, then there should be a crow’s foot on the payment end saying “merchandise can be paid through many payments” (probably true, but with a modification). And Payment should get a “what for” field indicating what it’s paying for.

Design check

Payment

Payment has MerchID, which was noted above as confusing. It also has a tight connection to Sale (makes sense: you pay for a sale somehow). It has a connction to LineItem, which in turn has a connection to Sale. That multiple-connection should be looked at.

I assert that payment isn’t about line items. When I get my sales receipt, it has line items on it. When I get my credit card statement, it only has things like date/store/amount: it does not generally include that level of detail. In other words: the payment is about the sale and the sale includes the line items.

3NF

Looking at it from a normal form perspective, Sale describes payment (“what is this for”); Date/type/info/billing address likewise describe the payment. Line Item describes the sale; Merch also describes something else. They are 3NF violations and need to be moved to their own tables. Fortunately those tables already exist.

Change

With both intuition and normal form practice suggesting a change, we can remove the connections from Payment and rely on Sale to model those.

Sale to LineItem relation

The biggest problem with this design is the “Item” attribute in Sale. It means a sale can only be for one item: there’s only one place for it to live. I think a Sale of multiple kinds of items should be possible.

If we make “Item” into “Items” in “Sale”, then that’s a 1NF violation.

But: we can have a many-to-one relation from LineItem to Sale by putting SaleID in LineItem:

Line Item

Line Item is looking pretty good. It brings together the Sale and the Mechandise, and it adds the number of Units being exchanged in the sale. That’s perfect.

In addition to LineItemID being the primary key in the LineItem entity, (SalesID, MerchID) could be a compound key: those two can describe a line item.

If you see that, then “Item Type” and “Inventory” describe only the Merch part of the (SalesID, MerchID) compound key. That’s a 2NF violation: this table is about “how many units are we selling in this order,” not about “do we ahve inventory [unrealted to this sale]” or “what do we call this thing?” (It would be odd if a product’s name changed with every sale.)

Those need to go to a different table: the Merchandise table that is the part of the key that they’re describing. But they’re already part of the Merchandise entity, so we just delete them and elimination the duplication:
